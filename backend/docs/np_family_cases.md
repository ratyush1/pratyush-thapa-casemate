# Family Law Cases — Nepal (National Civil Code 2074, DV Act 2066)

Jurisdiction: Nepal | Law: National Civil Code (Muluki Civil Sanhita) 2074, Domestic Violence Act 2066, Children's Act 2075

---

## Case: Want to get divorced — how to start the process

Divorce in Nepal is filed at the District Court of the district where you or your spouse currently lives. There are two types: mutual consent divorce and contested divorce. For mutual consent, both of you go to the District Court together and file a joint petition. The court will give a 3-month period for reconciliation. If both still agree after 3 months, the divorce decree is granted. This is the fastest and least expensive route.

What to do: Gather your marriage certificate, citizenship documents, and a written agreement covering property division, child custody, and maintenance (if applicable). Visit your District Court and file the petition with the court fee. A lawyer is not required for mutual consent divorce but is helpful for drafting the agreement.

---

## Case: Spouse refuses to divorce — contested divorce

If your spouse refuses to agree to divorce, you can still file a contested divorce petition in the District Court under the grounds listed in the National Civil Code 2074. Valid grounds include: physical or mental cruelty, abandonment for 3 or more years without reasonable cause, imprisonment for more than 3 years for a serious crime, conversion to another religion, or certain communicable diseases.

What to do: File your petition at the District Court with your marriage certificate, evidence supporting your ground for divorce (medical reports, police complaints, witness statements, or correspondence showing abandonment), and citizenship documents. The court will serve notice to your spouse to respond. The case will proceed through hearings. A contested divorce typically takes 1 to 3 years depending on complexity.

## Case: Child custody battle — who gets the children

Under the National Civil Code 2074, children below 5 years of age are presumed to be in the mother's custody unless the mother is found unfit by the court. For children above 5, the court decides based on the best interests of the child, considering the child's welfare, living conditions offered by each parent, emotional attachment, financial stability, and if the child is old enough, the child's own preference.

What to do: If you are seeking custody, file a petition in the District Court demonstrating your ability to care for the child — stable income, safe home environment, emotional bond, and availability. Gather supporting evidence: school records, medical records, character witnesses. The court may appoint a social worker to evaluate both parents' homes. Avoid making negative statements about the other parent in front of the child, as courts view this unfavorably.

## Case: Other parent took the child without permission

If your child's other parent has taken the child away without your consent or in violation of a court custody order, this is a serious situation. Under Nepal's Children's Act 2075, taking a child without the legal custodian's consent may constitute child abduction. If a custody order is violated, you can file for contempt of court.

What to do: File an urgent petition at the District Court for the return of the child (habeas corpus petition). Also file a police complaint. If there is an existing custody order being violated, show the court that order and request immediate enforcement. Act quickly — the longer the child remains away, the harder it may be to resolve.

## Case: Father not paying child maintenance — enforcement

If the court has ordered the other parent to pay child maintenance and they are refusing or simply not paying, this is a contempt of court. The court that issued the maintenance order can enforce it. The non-paying parent can face fines, attachment of salary or bank account, or even imprisonment for persistent non-compliance.

What to do: File an enforcement application in the same District Court that issued the maintenance order. Attach your copy of the order and evidence of non-payment (bank statements, written communications). Request an attachment order against the other parent's salary or assets. This process can move quickly when a clear court order already exists.

## Case: Domestic violence — what to do right now

Domestic violence is a criminal offense under Nepal's Domestic Violence (Crime and Punishment) Act 2066. You can get legal help immediately, even tonight. Go to the nearest police station and report the violence. Police can issue an emergency protection order instantly to stop the abuser from contacting or approaching you. You can also go directly to the District Court for a protection order.

You do NOT need to file for divorce first. You do NOT need to have physical injuries — emotional and psychological abuse counts. You do NOT need to go through a family member or mediator first.

What to do: Call Nepal Police on 100, or go directly to the nearest police station. Ask for Women and Children Service Center (WCSC) if available. Bring any evidence you have — photos of injuries, threatening messages, recordings. If you have children, they are covered by the protection order too. The National Women's Commission (01-4211735) can also assist.

---

## Case: Getting a restraining order / protection order

A protection order under the Domestic Violence Act 2066 can prohibit the abusive person from: physically approaching you, contacting you by any means, entering your home or workplace, and from making threats. Police can issue a temporary order within 24 hours. A longer-term court order is issued by the District Court.

What to do: File a written application at the police station or District Court describing the abuse and the protection you need. There is no filing fee for domestic violence protection orders. The order can be obtained urgently — within the same day in serious cases. Violating a protection order is itself a criminal offense.

## Case: Property division in divorce — who gets what

Under the National Civil Code 2074, marital property is divided equally (50-50) between spouses upon divorce. This includes property acquired together during the marriage, family businesses jointly managed, and assets in either spouse's name if acquired with joint income. Property owned by one spouse before the marriage, or property inherited or gifted specifically to one spouse, is generally excluded.

What to do: Make a list of all assets: property, vehicles, bank accounts, business shares, jewelry, and debts. If your spouse is hiding assets, request a court order for financial disclosure during divorce proceedings. The court has power to freeze assets to prevent them from being sold or transferred before division.

## Case: Spousal maintenance during and after divorce

The court can order a financially stronger spouse to pay maintenance to the financially dependent spouse both during the divorce proceedings (interim maintenance) and after divorce is granted (permanent maintenance). The amount depends on the paying spouse's income, the receiving spouse's financial needs, and the standard of living during marriage.

What to do: File an application for interim maintenance at the start of your divorce proceedings so you have financial support while the case runs. Bring evidence of your monthly expenses (rent, food, utilities, children's school fees) and evidence of your spouse's income (salary slips, business income, property ownership).

## Case: Inheritance rights — daughter vs son, equal rights

Under Nepal's National Civil Code 2074, sons and daughters have equal inheritance rights from their parents' property. A daughter cannot be disinherited simply because of her gender. If you are a daughter who has been excluded from inheritance or if your family refuses to give you your share, you have a legal right to claim it.

What to do: Send a formal written notice to the family requesting your share of inherited property. If refused, file a partition case (अंशबण्डा) at the District Court. You need to file within the limitation period — generally within 20 years of the deceased's death for property. Bring the deceased's citizenship, land certificates/lal purja, and your birth certificate proving your relationship.

## Case: Father or mother not paying maintenance despite court order

Enforcement of maintenance orders is unfortunately common as a problem in Nepal. If the paying parent is not complying: the court that issued the order can garnish (attach) their salary directly from their employer, freeze their bank account to release funds to you, or hold them in contempt which can lead to imprisonment.

What to do: Return to the District Court with your maintenance order and a written account of missed payments. Request an attachment of earning order so the employer directly sends the maintenance amount to you each month. This is typically the most reliable enforcement mechanism.

## Case: Marriage registration — how to register a marriage

Marriage registration in Nepal is done at the local Ward Office (वडा कार्यालय). You need to make an application with your citizenship documents, the citizenship of your spouse, two witness signatures, and passport-size photos. The fee is nominal. Registration should ideally happen before or at the time of marriage — late registration (after the marriage) is also possible but requires a fee and explanation.

What to do: Visit your local Ward Office with both spouses present, your citizenship documents, two witnesses, and photos. For Hindu marriages, the registration process is straightforward. For other religions or interfaith marriages, rules may vary slightly. A registered marriage certificate is legally important for all future matters — divorce, property, children's documents, passport applications.

## Case: Second marriage (bigamy) — someone married while first marriage was not dissolved

Under Nepal law, a person can only be in one marriage at a time. Marrying a second person while still legally married to the first (without a legal divorce decree) is bigamy, which is a criminal offense under the Muluki Criminal Code 2074. The second marriage is legally void.

What to do: If you are the first spouse, you can file a criminal complaint at the police station against your spouse for bigamy. You can also file a civil case in District Court to have the second marriage declared null and void, and to enforce your rights to marital property and maintenance.

## Case: Adoption process in Nepal

Adopting a child in Nepal requires approval from the District Court and the District Child Welfare Committee. Both married couples and single adults above 25 can apply. The adoptive parent must be at least 21 years older than the child. There must be no children already or a demonstrated reason for adoption. The biological parents or guardians must consent (or they must be legally unable to be located).

What to do: Contact the District Child Welfare Committee in your district to start the paperwork. You will need: citizenship documents, income proof, police clearance certificate, medical certificate, social investigation report (prepared by a social worker), and court approval. The process typically takes 6-12 months for domestic adoption. International (inter-country) adoption has additional requirements through the Department of Women, Children and Senior Citizens.

## Case: Missing spouse who has abandoned the family

If your spouse has abandoned you and your children for 3 or more years without reasonable cause and without maintenance, this is valid grounds for divorce under the National Civil Code 2074. You can file for divorce even if you cannot locate your spouse — the court will publish a public notice (newspaper advertisement) as a substitute for personal service.

What to do: Gather evidence of abandonment — any messages or lack of contact, your own financial records showing you have been supporting the family alone, and any witnesses. File your divorce petition at the District Court and inform them that your spouse cannot be located. The court will proceed after making effort to notify your spouse through public channels.

## Case: Daughter-in-law being thrown out of the house

A daughter-in-law who has been living in the family home after marriage and who does not have alternative housing cannot simply be thrown out by her in-laws. This may constitute domestic violence or harassment. The marital home is considered her residence, and she has a right to remain.

What to do: File a domestic violence complaint at the police station or District Court. You can also file a protection order application to stop your in-laws from forcing you out. If your husband is party to this, it strengthens the domestic violence claim. Organizations like Saathi, Tewa, or the National Women's Commission can provide immediate shelter and legal support.

## Case: Elder abuse — family member not caring for elderly parent

Under Nepal's Senior Citizens Act 2063, children are legally required to take care of their elderly parents. Failing to provide necessary care and neglecting elderly parents is legally actionable. Senior citizens can file a complaint through the Senior Citizens Protection Committee at the local Ward Office.

What to do: The elderly person can file a complaint at the Ward Office or District Court. Family mediators can be appointed before resorting to court. In serious cases, the court can order the responsible family member to pay maintenance to the elderly parent. Abuse or mistreatment can also be prosecuted as a criminal matter.

## Case: Divorce for Nepali couple married abroad / foreign marriage recognition

If you were married abroad and want to file for divorce in Nepal, you generally need to first get your foreign marriage recognized. Present your marriage certificate (apostilled or embassy-verified) at the Ward Office for registration. Once registered, you can file for divorce as normal at the District Court in Nepal.

What to do: Contact the Ministry of Foreign Affairs for guidance on recognizing your foreign marriage in Nepal. Once the marriage is registered domestically, proceed to the District Court for divorce proceedings. If your spouse is abroad and won't cooperate, the court can proceed using publication notice procedures.

## Case: Grandparents seeking visitation with grandchildren

Under Nepal's Children's Act 2075, grandparents may apply to the court for visitation rights if they are being denied access to their grandchildren. The court will consider what is in the best interests of the child. This is typically a District Court matter.

What to do: File a petition at the District Court explaining your relationship with the grandchildren, the importance of the relationship to the children's welfare, and the current denial of access. The court can issue a visitation order specifying dates and duration.
