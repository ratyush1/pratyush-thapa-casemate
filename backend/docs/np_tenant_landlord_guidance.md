# Tenant-Landlord Guidance (Nepal)

Last reviewed: 2026-03-14
Jurisdiction: Nepal
Law: House and Land Rent Control Act 2053 (1997), National Civil Code 2074

## Tenant rights in Nepal

Every tenant in Nepal has the right to a written rental agreement for lease periods beyond one month. The landlord must issue a receipt for every rent payment received. Tenants cannot be forcibly evicted without following the legal process, even after a lease expires.

Rent increases must be agreed upon in writing. In areas covered by rent control regulations, rent cannot be increased by more than 10% per year without tenant consent. If the landlord demands a higher increase, the tenant can refuse and seek intervention from the Ward Office.

## Eviction process and notice requirements

A landlord who wishes to end a tenancy must give at least 35 days written notice to the tenant. The notice must state the reason for eviction and must be delivered in person or by registered letter. Valid reasons for eviction include: non-payment of rent for 2 or more consecutive months, serious damage to the property by the tenant, using the property for illegal purposes, or the landlord's genuine personal need to occupy the property.

A landlord cannot lock out or forcibly remove a tenant without a court order. Forcible eviction without following the legal process is illegal and the tenant can file a complaint at the Ward Office or approach the District Court for an injunction order immediately.

## Tenant remedies for landlord violations

If the landlord refuses to issue rent receipts, the tenant should send a written demand by letter and keep a copy. If the landlord illegally withholds the security deposit after the tenant vacates, the tenant can file a claim at the Ward Office or District Court within 35 days of the return date. If the landlord threatens forced eviction or shuts off utilities, the tenant can file an urgent petition for protection in the District Court.

## Landlord remedies for tenant violations

If the tenant fails to pay rent for 2 or more months, the landlord must first issue a written demand notice giving 15 days to pay. If unpaid, the landlord can file an eviction petition at the Ward Office or District Court. The court will schedule a hearing and the tenant has the right to respond. The court may issue a possession order after the hearing. The full eviction process through court typically takes 2 to 6 months.

## Security deposit

A landlord may collect a security deposit of up to 3 months rent. The deposit must be returned to the tenant within 30 days of vacating, minus any deductions for damages beyond normal wear and tear. Withholding the deposit without valid reason is a civil wrong that can be remedied through the court.

## Documents to gather

Gather the following: written rental agreement or lease, rent payment receipts, bank transfer records, photographs of the property condition, eviction or notice letter received, any correspondence with the landlord, and witness contact details if disputes are ongoing.

## Next steps

If you receive an eviction notice, check whether it gives the required 35 days and states a valid reason. If the notice is defective or the reason is not valid, you can contest it at the Ward Office within 15 days. If facing immediate forced eviction or lockout, go to the District Court on an urgent basis and file for an interim injunction to remain in possession while the dispute is resolved.

## Safety note

This is general legal information and not legal advice. For your specific situation, consulting a licensed lawyer or the nearest Nepal Bar Association legal aid desk is recommended.
