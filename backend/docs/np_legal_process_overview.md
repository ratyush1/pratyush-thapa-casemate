# Nepal Legal Process Overview

Last reviewed: 2026-03-14
Jurisdiction: Nepal
Law: Muluki Civil Sanhita 2074, Muluki Criminal Procedure Code 2074, Court Management Act

## Court hierarchy in Nepal

Nepal's court system has three levels: District Courts at the base handle most civil and criminal cases at first instance; High Courts in each province hear appeals; and the Supreme Court of Nepal is the final appellate court and constitutional court.

For most everyday legal disputes — employment, family, property, contract — you file at the District Court of the district where the dispute arose or where the defendant resides. The correct forum is important; filing in the wrong court may result in dismissal.

## Civil case process

To start a civil case, you file a written petition (darbhandi) at the District Court office with the required court fee, your identification, and supporting documents. The court issues a summons to the opposite party, who has 30 days to file a written response. After the response, the court schedules hearings. Either party can apply for interim relief (such as an injunction or asset freeze) at any stage before final order.

The entire civil case process at the District Court typically takes 6 months to 2 years depending on complexity and backlogs. After a District Court judgment, a party has 35 days to appeal to the High Court, and then 35 days to file a further appeal to the Supreme Court on a question of law.

## Limitation periods for civil claims

Claims must be filed within the legal limitation period or the court will not accept them. For contract disputes the general limitation period is 5 years from the date of breach. For property and tenancy disputes it is generally within 35 days for urgent possession matters, and up to 2 years for compensation claims. For employment claims, the complaint at the Labor Office must be filed within 35 days of the event. Missing the limitation deadline permanently bars your claim.

## Mediation and alternative dispute resolution

Under the Mediation Act 2068, courts in Nepal are required to refer many civil cases to mediation first before proceeding to full hearing. Mediation is conducted by trained mediators and is faster and cheaper than full litigation. If mediation succeeds, the settlement agreement has the force of a court decree. If mediation fails, the case returns to court.

## Legal aid

Free legal aid is available to individuals who cannot afford a lawyer under the Legal Aid Act 1997. The District Legal Aid Committee in each district provides legal assistance including representation. You can apply at the District Court office or contact the District Bar Association for referral.

## How to choose the right type of lawyer

For employment disputes, look for a lawyer specializing in labor law. For family matters including divorce and custody, choose a family or civil lawyer. For property and tenancy problems, a property law or civil lawyer is appropriate. For criminal defense, you need a criminal law specialist. ## Urgency indicators requiring immediate legal help

You need a lawyer urgently if: you have received a court summons with a hearing date, you have been arrested or are in police custody, you face forced eviction or lockout, there is a child safety emergency, you have a filing deadline within 35 days that you have not met, or you have received a restraining order.

## Safety note

This is general legal information and not legal advice. For your specific situation, consulting a licensed lawyer or the nearest Nepal Bar Association legal aid desk is recommended.
