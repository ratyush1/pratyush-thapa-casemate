# Family Dispute Guidance (Nepal)

Last reviewed: 2026-03-14
Jurisdiction: Nepal
Law: National Civil Code 2074 (Muluki Civil Sanhita), Domestic Violence (Crime and Punishment) Act 2066

## Marriage and divorce under Nepal's National Civil Code 2074

Divorce in Nepal is governed by Part 2, Chapter 4 of the National Civil Code 2074. Either spouse may petition for divorce from the District Court having jurisdiction over the matrimonial home or the wife's current residence.

Mutual consent divorce: Both spouses may jointly file a petition in District Court. The court will typically wait 3 months for reconciliation before granting the decree. This is the fastest and least contested route.

Contested divorce grounds include: physical or mental cruelty, abandonment for more than 3 years without reasonable cause, conversion to another religion, imprisonment for more than 3 years for a serious offense, and certain communicable diseases. The court considers evidence submitted by both parties before granting a decree.

## Property division in divorce

Under the National Civil Code 2074, marital property is divided equally (50/50) between spouses upon divorce. Property acquired before marriage or gifted to one spouse individually is generally excluded from division. Each spouse must disclose all assets they hold individually and jointly.

## Maintenance and alimony

The court may award maintenance to financially dependent spouses during and after divorce proceedings. The amount is determined based on the income of both parties, number of dependents, and cost of living. Maintenance can be revised by the court if circumstances change significantly.

## Child custody and visitation

Child custody decisions under the National Civil Code 2074 are based on the best interests of the child. Children below 5 years of age are generally placed in the mother's custody unless she is found to be unfit. For children above 5 years, the court considers the child's welfare, attachments, living conditions of each parent, and the child's own preference if the child is mature enough to express it. Both parents retain visitation rights unless the court finds a safety reason to restrict contact.

## Child maintenance

The non-custodial parent must pay child maintenance. The amount is set by the court based on the child's needs and the parent's financial capacity. Failure to pay court-ordered child maintenance can result in contempt proceedings and enforcement through the court.

## Domestic violence and protection orders

Domestic violence is a criminal offense under the Domestic Violence (Crime and Punishment) Act 2066. A victim can file a complaint at the nearest police station, District Court, or through the National Women Commission. Police can issue a temporary protection order within 24 hours of receiving the complaint. The District Court can issue a longer-term restraining order prohibiting the perpetrator from contacting or approaching the victim. Offenders face imprisonment of up to 6 months and fines for first offense, with harsher penalties for repeat offenses.

## Documents to gather

Gather the following: marriage certificate or registration document, proof of residence, evidence of abuse (photos, medical reports, witness statements), financial documents (salary slips, bank statements, property ownership records), children's birth certificates, any prior court orders related to the family.

## Next steps

If you need to file for divorce, visit the District Court in your district with your marriage certificate and a written petition. If you are facing domestic violence, go to the nearest police station or the Women and Children Service Center (महिला तथा बालबालिका सेवा केन्द्र) for immediate protection. If child custody or maintenance is urgently needed before the divorce is finalized, you can apply for interim orders from the District Court.

## Safety note

This is general legal information and not legal advice. For your specific situation, consulting a licensed lawyer or the nearest Nepal Bar Association legal aid desk is recommended.
