# Employment Law Cases — Nepal (Labor Act 2074)

Jurisdiction: Nepal | Law: Labor Act 2074, Social Security Act 2074, Bonus Act 2030

---

## Case: Fired or terminated without notice

If your employer fired you without giving 30 days advance written notice, or without paying one month's salary in lieu of notice, that is a violation of Section 50 of the Labor Act 2074. You are entitled to receive either the 30-day notice period or one month's full salary as compensation. This applies whether you were a permanent, temporary, or contract worker.

What to do: Write a letter to your employer asking for your notice pay in writing. If they refuse or ignore you, file a complaint at the nearest District Labor Office within 35 days of your termination date. Bring your appointment letter, salary slips, and the termination letter they gave you. The Labor Office can order your employer to pay you.

---

## Case: Employer not paying salary — unpaid wages

If your employer has not paid your salary for one month or more, this is a direct violation of the Labor Act 2074 which requires salaries to be paid within one month of the due date. You do not have to keep waiting. You have the right to file a complaint at the District Labor Office.

What to do: First send a written demand to your employer asking for your unpaid salary with a 7-day deadline. Keep a copy. If they still don't pay, file at the Labor Office immediately. You must file within 35 days of the salary becoming due. Bring your employment letter and any salary slips you have. The Labor Office can order full payment plus interest.

---

## Case: Wrongful dismissal — fired without valid reason

Under Chapter 10 of Nepal's Labor Act 2074, an employer can only terminate a permanent employee for specific valid reasons such as serious misconduct, inability to perform duties, or retrenchment due to genuine business need. Firing someone because the employer dislikes them, to avoid paying benefits, or out of personal reasons is considered unfair dismissal.

If you were unfairly dismissed, you can file for reinstatement or compensation at the Labor Office. Compensation for unfair dismissal can include back wages for the period you were unemployed. File within 35 days of termination. The Labor Court can order reinstatement if the dismissal was found to be baseless.

## Case: Gratuity not paid after leaving job

Gratuity is a mandatory payment under the Labor Act 2074 that every employee who has completed at least one year of service is entitled to receive when they leave. It is calculated at one month's basic salary for every year of service (or at the Social Security Fund equivalent rate). Many employers try to avoid paying this.

What to do: Calculate your gratuity yourself: take your monthly basic salary and multiply by the number of years you worked. Send a written demand to your employer. If unpaid within 30 days, file at the District Labor Office. You must file within 35 days of your last working day or the date payment was due. The employer has no legal right to withhold gratuity if you have worked for at least one year.

## Case: Provident fund not deposited by employer

Under the Social Security Fund Act 2074, employers are required to contribute 20% of each employee's basic salary to the Social Security Fund (SSF) every month (employer contributes 20%, employee contributes 11%). If your employer deducted your contribution from your salary but did not deposit it to SSF, that is both a labor violation and potentially fraud.

What to do: Check your SSF account online at ssf.gov.np to see if contributions were deposited. If contributions are missing, file a complaint at the Social Security Fund office and also at the District Labor Office. The employer faces penalties and must deposit all missing contributions with interest.

## Case: Overtime hours not paid

Under the Labor Act 2074, any work beyond 8 hours per day or 48 hours per week is overtime and must be paid at 150% of your regular hourly rate. If your employer makes you work overtime but pays you nothing extra or your regular rate only, that is a violation.

What to do: Keep a personal record of your overtime hours with dates and times. Request payslips from your employer in writing. File a complaint at the Labor Office for unpaid overtime within 35 days of each incident (or from the date you last requested payment). The Labor Office can calculate and order full overtime payment.

## Case: Annual leave or sick leave refused

Every employee in Nepal who has completed one year of service is entitled to 18 days of paid annual leave per year under the Labor Act 2074. You are also entitled to 12 days of paid sick leave per year (with a medical certificate for absences over 3 days). An employer cannot refuse to give you these leaves if you are entitled to them.

What to do: Submit your leave application in writing and keep a copy. If your employer refuses and there is no legitimate business reason, file a written complaint with your employer's human resources or management. If still denied, file at the Labor Office. Note that annual leave can sometimes be carried forward and encashed upon leaving if unused.

## Case: Maternity leave refused — pregnant employee terminated

This is a serious violation. Under the Labor Act 2074, a female employee who has worked for at least 180 days is entitled to 98 days of maternity leave (60 days paid, 38 days unpaid). More importantly, an employer is strictly prohibited from terminating a female employee because of pregnancy or during maternity leave. Doing so is both a labor law violation and a form of gender discrimination.

What to do: File an urgent complaint at the District Labor Office immediately. You may also file at the National Women Commission. Bring proof of your employment, your pregnancy documentation, and the termination notice. You are entitled to reinstatement and back wages. Do not delay — file within 35 days.

## Case: Sexual harassment at workplace

Sexual harassment at the workplace is prohibited under the Labor Act 2074 and the Workplace Sexual Harassment Prevention Act. Every organization with 5 or more employees is required to have an internal complaint committee to handle harassment complaints. If your employer does not have one, that itself is a violation.

What to do: If a committee exists, file your complaint in writing with the committee within 3 months of the incident. The committee must investigate and give a decision within 3 months. If the committee does not exist, does not act, or you are not satisfied with the outcome, file directly at the District Labor Office or the District Court. Keep all evidence — messages, emails, dates, and witness names.

## Case: Psychological harassment, bullying, or hostile work environment

Mental harassment, bullying, and creating a hostile work environment are forms of workplace harassment covered under the Labor Act 2074. Repeated humiliation, threats, excessive criticism, isolation, or verbal abuse by a supervisor or colleague qualifies as harassment even if it is not physical.

What to do: Document every incident in writing with date, time, location, and what was said or done. If there is a grievance committee, file with them first. If the employer ignores the issue, file at the District Labor Office. In serious cases, you can also file a police complaint for criminal harassment under the Muluki Criminal Code 2074.

## Case: No written employment contract given

Under Section 8 of the Labor Act 2074, every employer must provide a written employment contract at the time of hiring. Working without a written contract does not remove your labor rights — you are still entitled to all legal minimum benefits. But it makes it harder to prove the terms agreed upon.

What to do: Formally request a written contract from your employer in a letter or email and keep a copy. If they refuse, this is itself a labor law violation and can be reported at the Labor Office. Even without a contract, you can show proof of employment through salary slips, bank transfers, ID card, or witness statements.

## Case: Forced to resign — employer pressuring resignation

If your employer is threatening you, withholding salary, or making working conditions so intolerable that you feel forced to resign, this may qualify as constructive dismissal under Nepal labor law. A resignation under duress or pressure does not legally protect the employer from their obligations.

What to do: Do not sign any resignation letter under pressure. If you have already resigned under duress, file a complaint at the Labor Office within 35 days explaining the circumstances. Gather evidence of the pressure — messages, emails, or witness statements. You may be entitled to the same remedies as an unfairly dismissed employee.

## Case: Bonus not paid — employer denying annual bonus

Under the Bonus Act 2030, employees in profit-making organizations are entitled to receive an annual bonus of a minimum of 8.33% of their annual basic salary. Employers cannot simply refuse to pay bonus without legally valid financial justification (proven losses). If your employer is making a profit but not paying bonuses, you have a legal claim.

What to do: Request the bonus payment in writing with the calculation. If unpaid, file a complaint at the Labor Office. Employers are required to post certified profit/loss statements — you can request these through the Labor Office investigation process.

## Case: Training bond — signed an agreement, want to leave

Many employers ask employees to sign training bonds agreeing to work for a set period after sponsored training or to repay training costs. Under Nepal labor law, such bonds can be enforced by the employer if the training cost was genuine and significant. However, the amount claimed cannot be unreasonable, and the bond cannot prevent you from leaving.

What to do: Review the exact terms of the bond you signed — the amount, the training, and the repayment clause. If you want to leave, you may owe a proportionate amount based on how much of the bond period you served. Consult a lawyer before leaving or signing anything. The Labor Office can mediate bond disputes if the employer is making an unfair or exaggerated claim.

## Case: Employer not giving work experience or service letter

Every employer is required to issue a service letter (experience letter) when an employee leaves, confirming the period of service, position, and nature of work. Refusing to issue this is becoming common and is a violation of the Labor Act.

What to do: Submit a formal written request for your service letter. If the employer refuses without reason, file a complaint at the District Labor Office. The Labor Office can direct the employer to issue the letter.

## Case: Workplace accident or injury — employer not taking responsibility

If you are injured during the course of your work, your employer is required to provide immediate medical treatment, compensate for treatment costs, and compensate for disability or death under the Labor Act 2074 and the Social Security Act. Refusing to pay medical costs or denying that the accident was work-related is a violation.

What to do: Report the accident to your employer in writing immediately and get a copy. Get medical treatment and keep all receipts. File a report at the Labor Office and with the SSF (if you are covered). If the employer refuses compensation, file a formal labor complaint. In cases of serious injury or death, a criminal negligence complaint at the police station may also be appropriate.

## Case: Employer discriminating based on gender, caste, or religion

Discrimination at the workplace based on gender, caste, ethnicity, religion, or disability is prohibited under the Labor Act 2074 and Nepal's Constitution. This includes unequal pay for the same work, refusal to promote, or different treatment based on these characteristics.

What to do: Document all instances of discriminatory treatment with dates, specifics, and any witnesses. File a complaint at the District Labor Office. For caste-based discrimination specifically, you may also file under the Caste Discrimination and Untouchability (Offence and Punishment) Act 2068, which is a criminal offense with up to 3 years imprisonment.

## Case: Non-compete clause — employer threatening if you join competitor

Non-compete agreements restrict employees from joining competitor companies after leaving. Under Nepal law, non-compete clauses that are overly broad, unreasonably long in duration, or that prevent you from earning a livelihood may be considered unenforceable as they violate fundamental rights.

What to do: Consult a lawyer before accepting a new job. Review the scope, duration, and geographic restriction in your non-compete clause. If the clause is reasonable and you signed it knowingly, some courts may enforce it. If it is broad or unreasonable, you have grounds to challenge it. The employer cannot criminally prosecute you for joining a competitor — they can only pursue a civil damages claim.

## Case: Retrenchment — company laying off employees

An employer can retrench (lay off) employees due to genuine business closure, downsizing, or technological change under Section 58 of the Labor Act 2074. However, they must follow the process: 30 days advance notice, payment of one month's salary for each year of service as severance, and priority rehiring if the company expands again.

What to do: If you are retrenched, calculate your severance entitlement (1 month salary per year of service) and ensure it is paid fully. If retrenchment is being used as a pretext to target specific employees or avoid paying benefits, file a complaint at the Labor Office — the burden of proof is on the employer to show genuine business need.

---

## Case: Probation period — terminated during probation

Many employers use the probation period to avoid labor law protections. Under the Labor Act 2074, even during probation (which can be up to 6 months), an employee must receive minimum wage, and termination must be for a valid reason and with notice. You are protected against arbitrary dismissal even during probation.

What to do: If terminated during probation without a genuine reason or without any notice, file a complaint at the Labor Office. The employer must show a valid performance or conduct reason for probation termination.
