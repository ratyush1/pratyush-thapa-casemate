# Criminal Law Cases — Nepal (Muluki Criminal Code 2074, Criminal Procedure Code 2074)

Jurisdiction: Nepal | Law: Muluki Criminal Code 2074, Muluki Criminal Procedure Code 2074, Electronic Transaction Act 2063

---

## Case: How to file an FIR (First Information Report)

An FIR (First Information Report / kasursambandhi jaaheri) is the starting point for any criminal case in Nepal. You file it at the nearest police station. Go to the duty officer, describe what happened in detail, and request it to be registered. The police must give you a copy of the registered FIR. There is no fee for filing an FIR.

What to bring: Written summary of events (dates, times, location, what happened, names of accused if known), any evidence (photos, screenshots, documents), your citizenship or ID. For online fraud, bring screenshots of all transactions and communications. For theft, bring a list of stolen items with estimated values.

What to do if police refuse to register your FIR: This is unfortunately common. If police refuse without valid reason, you can file a direct complaint to the District Court (written application called tirsha darbhandi). The court can direct police to register and investigate. You can also file a complaint with the District Police Office about the refusal.

## Case: Falsely accused of a crime — wrongful accusation

If someone has filed a false complaint or FIR against you, this is a serious situation that requires immediate legal action. Under Nepal law, filing a false police complaint is itself a criminal offense. However, you must first deal with the investigation or case pending against you.

What to do: Contact a criminal defense lawyer immediately. During police questioning, you have the right to remain silent (Article 20 of the Constitution of Nepal). Do not sign any statement without your lawyer reviewing it. Gather evidence that contradicts the accusations: alibi witnesses, phone records, CCTV footage, documentary proof. File a counter-complaint for false reporting against the person who filed against you if you have evidence it was deliberate.

## Case: Arrested by police — what are my rights

If you or someone you know is arrested, these rights apply immediately under the Constitution of Nepal (Article 20) and the Muluki Criminal Procedure Code 2074:
• You must be told the reason for arrest immediately.
• You must be produced before a magistrate within 24 hours of arrest (excluding travel time).
• You have the right to consult a lawyer before your first interrogation.
• You have the right to remain silent during interrogation.
• You cannot be kept in police custody for more than 24 hours without a court order.
• If you are a woman, you may only be searched by a female officer and should not be detained at a police station overnight without a female officer present.

What to do: Immediately request to call a family member or lawyer. Do not make any statement or sign anything before consulting a lawyer. Have a family member file for bail at the District Court as soon as you are produced before the magistrate.

## Case: Applying for bail — bailable offense

In Nepal, offenses are classified as bailable or non-bailable. For bailable offenses (most minor offenses — petty theft, minor assault, traffic offenses, etc.), bail must be granted as a matter of right when you first appear before the magistrate. Police cannot hold you in jail for a bailable offense once bail conditions are met.

What to do: When first produced before a magistrate, your lawyer should immediately apply for bail. For bailable offenses, the court will grant bail upon furnishing a personal guarantee (dhakali/surety). The surety can be from a family member or property owner. If bail conditions are met and the court still refuses, you can appeal to the High Court immediately.

---

## Case: Bail for serious crimes — murder, rape, fraud, drugs

For serious non-bailable offenses, bail is at the court's discretion based on the nature of the crime, the likelihood of the accused fleeing, risk to witnesses, and the strength of evidence. The court can set bail with conditions like surrendering your passport, reporting to the police station regularly, or a high cash bond.

What to do: Engage a criminal lawyer immediately. At the first hearing the lawyer should file a formal bail application presenting reasons why bail conditions can be safely set — stable family ties in Nepal, employment, no prior criminal record, willingness to surrender passport. If bail is denied, it can be renewed when circumstances change, or appealed to the High Court. Challenging the grounds for remand extension is also an important tool.

## Case: Police remand — how long can they hold you

Police can apply to the District Court for a remand order to hold you in custody for investigation. The Muluki Criminal Procedure Code 2074 limits total remand to 25 days for most serious offenses. For each remand period (typically 5-7 days at a time), police must appear before the court to justify continued custody. You must be produced before the court for each extension.

What to do: Your lawyer must appear at every remand hearing to challenge the need for further custody. If the police have already conducted their investigation, there is no justification for further remand. The court can deny extension of remand. The detained person must be physically produced before the court — if not produced, challenge this immediately.

## Case: Cheque bounce — my cheque bounced, what happens

In Nepal, issuing a cheque that bounces (is not honored by the bank) is a criminal offense under the Banking Offense and Punishment Act 2064. If a cheque bounced because there were insufficient funds, the payee can file a criminal complaint at the District Court. The cheque issuer can face imprisonment up to 3 years and a fine.

If you are the payee (victim): Send a formal demand notice to the cheque issuer giving 35 days to pay. If they do not pay, file a criminal complaint at the District Court or the Commercial Court with the original bounced cheque, the bank's return memo, and your demand notice. The court will summon the issuer. This process can recover the amount through criminal prosecution.

If you are the accused: The best option is to pay the cheque amount or negotiate a settlement before the case goes to trial. The criminal case can be settled by bringing evidence of payment or a written settlement agreement.

## Case: Online fraud / internet scam — lost money online

Online financial fraud includes: fake job offers asking for fees, romance scams, fake shopping websites, fraudulent bank messages (phishing), and investment scams. These are criminal offenses under the Electronic Transaction Act 2063 and the Muluki Criminal Code 2074.

What to do: File an FIR at the nearest police station AND at Nepal Police's Cyber Crime Bureau (Cyber Bureau Nepal, Teku, Kathmandu, helpline 1930). Bring screenshots of all communications, payment transactions, bank statements, and the website or social media profile used by the fraudster. The Cyber Bureau has tools to trace online fraud that local police stations may not. File as quickly as possible — digital evidence can disappear.

## Case: Blackmail or extortion — someone threatening me for money

Blackmail (using threatening communications to demand money or other things) and extortion are serious criminal offenses under sections 231-240 of the Muluki Criminal Code 2074, with penalties up to 5 years imprisonment. This includes threatening messages, threats to release private photos or videos, and threats of physical harm unless payment is made.

What to do: Do NOT pay the blackmailer — paying encourages them to demand more. Do NOT delete the threatening messages — they are crucial evidence. Screenshot and save all communications. File a complaint at the nearest police station and optionally the Cyber Crime Bureau if the threats are online. Request police protection if you feel physically at risk. The police can trace and arrest the blackmailer.

## Case: Defamation — someone spreading false rumors or statements about me

Under the Muluki Criminal Code 2074, intentionally making a false statement about a person that damages their reputation is defamation, which is both a criminal offense (Section 305-307) and a civil wrong. This includes false statements in person, in newspapers, on social media, or in official communications.

What to do: Document the defamatory statements with screenshots, recordings, newspaper copies, or witnesses. Send a legal notice to the person through a lawyer demanding they stop and retract their statements. If they continue, file a criminal complaint at the police station AND a civil case in the District Court for damages. For social media defamation, the Cyber Bureau can help identify anonymous accounts.

## Case: Physical assault — someone hit or attacked me

Physical assault (dhakka, pito, sutna) is covered under Section 121-155 of the Muluki Criminal Code 2074. The severity depends on the injuries — simple assault, grievous hurt, or attempted murder. You have both criminal (FIR) and civil (compensation) remedies.

What to do: Get medical treatment first and obtain a medical certificate documenting your injuries. File an FIR at the nearest police station while your injuries are still visible. The police must investigate and can arrest the accused. You may also apply for compensation from the accused as part of the criminal proceedings. Keep all medical records and bills.

## Case: Theft complaint — something was stolen from me

Theft is covered in Sections 295-302 of the Muluki Criminal Code 2074. For minor theft, penalties are proportional to the value. Major theft, robbery, and dacoity carry longer sentences. You can both file a criminal complaint and claim compensation for stolen property.

What to do: File an FIR immediately at the nearest police station with a detailed list of the stolen items and their approximate values. For house burglary, do not touch the crime scene before police arrive (preserve fingerprints/evidence). For vehicle theft, also report to the Traffic Police. For digital theft (bank account, cryptocurrency), report to the Cyber Bureau. Note that stolen goods worth below a certain value may be treated as petty theft with lighter penalties.

## Case: Cyberbullying or online harassment

Threatening, humiliating, or harassing someone online, in chat groups, or through social media is an offense under the Electronic Transaction Act 2063 and the Muluki Criminal Code. Sharing someone's private photographs or videos without their consent (revenge porn) is a specific offense with penalties up to 5 years imprisonment.

What to do: Screenshot and save all harassment content with dates. Do NOT delete the evidence. File a complaint at Nepal Police's Cyber Crime Bureau (1930) or visit them in person in Teku, Kathmandu. Local police stations can also accept the complaint and forward it to the Cyber Bureau. Provide the platform name, username, and account details of the harasser.

## Case: Drug charge — found with drugs

Being found in possession of drugs in Nepal is treated seriously under the Narcotic Drugs (Control) Act 2033. Penalties depend on the type and quantity of drug. Possession of small amounts for personal use may result in a lighter sentence or compulsory treatment program. Trafficking, distribution, or large quantity possession carries much heavier penalties including long imprisonment.

What to do: Do not resist arrest. Do not make statements without a lawyer. Engage a criminal defense lawyer immediately. The lawyer will examine: was the search conducted legally, was there a lawful warrant, is the quantity consistent with personal use, and were your rights observed? These procedural points can significantly affect the outcome. Cooperation with treatment programs (for personal use cases) may be considered as a mitigating factor.

## Case: Traffic accident — someone hit my vehicle or I was in an accident

A traffic accident resulting in injury or significant vehicle damage requires a police report. Traffic offenses and accident liability are governed by the Vehicle and Transport Management Act 2074. For accidents involving injury, the driver at fault may face both criminal charges (negligent driving) and civil liability for compensation.

What to do: Call the Traffic Police immediately (103). Do not move vehicles until police arrive if someone is injured. Get the other driver's license and vehicle registration. Get witness names and contact numbers. Seek medical treatment and keep all bills. The insurance company needs a police report. If the other driver was negligent and denies responsibility, file a formal complaint with the Traffic Police. Compensation can be claimed through the Motor Third Party insurance scheme.

## Case: Corruption complaint against a government official

Corruption, bribery, and misuse of authority by government officials are offenses under the Prevention of Corruption Act 2059. Victims of corruption or witnesses can report to the Commission for the Investigation of Abuse of Authority (CIAA), which is Nepal's anti-corruption body.

What to do: File a complaint at the CIAA (ciaa.gov.np or their toll-free line 1110). Your complaint should describe the official, the corrupt act, the amount demanded or paid, and any witnesses or documentation. The CIAA investigates and can prosecute government officials. Speaking to the CIAA is confidential and you are protected from retaliation.

## Case: Victim of trafficking / forced labor

Human trafficking and transportation is one of the most serious crimes in Nepal under the Human Trafficking and Transportation (Control) Act 2064, with penalties up to life imprisonment for traffickers. Victims of trafficking have the right to full legal protection, shelter, medical care, and compensation.

What to do: Contact the police or the National Human Rights Commission immediately. Anti-trafficking organizations like Maiti Nepal (01-4470101) provide shelter, rescue support, and legal assistance. Victims are not criminally liable for actions committed while being trafficked. The reintegration and support program is available through the Department of Women, Children and Senior Citizens.

## Case: Right to compensation as a crime victim

Under the Muluki Criminal Code 2074, victims of crime have the right to receive compensation from the perpetrator as part of the criminal sentence. The court can order the convicted person to pay compensation to the victim for medical costs, psychological harm, lost income, and property damage.

What to do: When your FIR case goes to court, file a separate compensation application through your lawyer or directly with the court. Keep all receipts and documentation of your losses (medical bills, repair costs, income loss with salary slips). The court will determine the compensation amount as part of sentencing.

## Case: False FIR against me — what to do

If someone has filed a false or malicious complaint against you, act calmly and strategically. Under the Muluki Criminal Code, filing a false complaint to harm someone is itself a criminal offense.

What to do: Cooperate with police but do not give a statement without your lawyer. Gather your alibi and all evidence contradicting the allegations. Your lawyer can file an application to the investigating police officer challenging the legality or factual basis of the complaint. Simultaneously, prepare a counter-complaint for false reporting against the complainant. Once you are cleared, file the counter-complaint and potentially a defamation civil case for the reputational damage caused.
