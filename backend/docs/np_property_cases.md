# Property and Tenancy Cases — Nepal (House Rent Act 2053, National Civil Code 2074)

Jurisdiction: Nepal | Law: House and Land Rent Control Act 2053, National Civil Code 2074, Land Act 2021

---

## Case: Landlord threatening to throw me out — illegal eviction

A landlord who wants a tenant to vacate must give at least 35 days written notice. Without this notice, any attempt to force you out is illegal. The landlord cannot change the locks, remove your belongings, or cut off utilities to force you out. Doing so is illegal and you can report it to the police immediately as illegal eviction.

What to do: If the landlord is threatening you right now, call the nearest police station (100) and report it. You can also file an urgent petition at the District Court for an interim protection order to remain in possession of the property while the dispute is resolved. The court can issue this order within a day or two in urgent cases. Keep all messages, notices, and communications from the landlord as evidence.

---

## Case: Landlord changed locks or locked me out

If your landlord has changed the locks while your belongings are inside or while you still have a valid tenancy, this is an illegal act. It is both a civil wrong (you can claim damages and re-entry) and potentially a criminal offense. You do not have to accept this.

What to do: Go to the nearest police station immediately with your rental agreement or proof of tenancy (receipts, messages, bank transfer records for rent). Police can assist you in regaining entry. Simultaneously, file an urgent petition at the District Court for an injunction ordering the landlord to restore your access. Document all damages to your belongings.

---

## Case: Landlord refusing to return security deposit

The landlord is required to return your security deposit within 30 days of you vacating the property, minus any legitimate deductions for damage beyond normal wear and tear. Refusing to return the deposit without showing documented evidence of actual damage is a violation of the House Rent Act.

What to do: Send a formal written demand to the landlord by registered letter requesting the full deposit (or itemized deductions with receipts). If unpaid within 15 days, file a complaint at the Ward Office first, and if unresolved, at the District Court. Take photographs of the property when you vacate as evidence of its condition. Witnesses at the time of handover are also helpful.

## Case: Landlord demanding huge rent increase

Under the House and Land Rent Control Act, rent increases must be agreed upon in writing. In rent-controlled areas, rent cannot be increased by more than 10% per year without tenant consent. Even outside rent-controlled areas, a landlord cannot unilaterally demand an excessive increase mid-tenancy — they must give notice and negotiate.

What to do: Check if your area is designated as a rent-controlled area (major cities typically are). Review your rental agreement for any increase clause. If the landlord is demanding more than 10% or more than what your agreement allows, refuse in writing. File a complaint at the Ward Office if the landlord retaliates by threatening eviction. You cannot be evicted for refusing an illegal rent increase.

---

## Case: No written lease agreement — landlord now denying tenancy

If you have been paying rent but never signed a written lease, your tenancy is still legally recognized as long as you can prove payment. Receipts, bank transfer records, messages, or witnesses are all valid evidence of a tenancy. A landlord who accepted rent from you cannot pretend you were never their tenant.

What to do: Gather all evidence of your payments — bank transfer records, WhatsApp messages confirming rent, witnesses, photographs of the property and your belongings inside. File a complaint at the Ward Office if the landlord is trying to evict you while denying a tenancy relationship. Future payments should always be by bank transfer with a note stating "rent for [property] [month]."

## Case: Landlord entering property without permission

Once a property is rented to a tenant, it is the tenant's private space. The landlord cannot enter without giving reasonable advance notice (typically 24 hours except in emergencies). Entering without permission is a trespass.

What to do: Send a written message to the landlord reminding them that advance notice is required before entry. Document all unauthorized entries with dates and times. If the landlord continues, file a complaint at the Ward Office. In cases where the landlord is filming you, harassing you, or making you feel unsafe inside your home, you can also report it as harassment at the police station.

---

## Case: Landlord not doing necessary repairs

While tenants are responsible for minor day-to-day maintenance, major structural repairs — roof leaks, broken plumbing, electrical failures, broken windows — are the landlord's responsibility. A landlord who refuses to make essential repairs is in breach of the tenancy agreement.

What to do: Send a written notice to the landlord specifying the repairs needed and a reasonable deadline (15-21 days). Get written estimates from contractors. If the landlord ignores your request, file a complaint at the Ward Office. Keep records of the damage with photographs. In some cases, if the property becomes uninhabitable due to the landlord's negligence, you may be entitled to withhold rent or terminate the tenancy and claim compensation.

## Case: Land title dispute — two parties claiming same land

Land title disputes (lal purja disputes) are handled by the Land Revenue Office and District Courts. Common situations include two people holding documents for the same plot, heirs disputing inherited land, fraudulent transfer of land, or boundary encroachment. This is one of the most common civil cases in Nepal.

What to do: Gather all documents: your lal purja (land ownership certificate), registration paper, field map (naksha), and purchase deed if applicable. File a case at the Land Revenue Office for administrative matters or at the District Court for legal title disputes. An immediate option is to file a lis pendens (land freeze) request to prevent the other party from selling or transferring the land while the dispute is pending.

## Case: Neighbor encroaching on my land / boundary dispute

If a neighbor has built on, fenced, or is claiming part of your land, this is a boundary encroachment. Nepal's Land Act and the Land Revenue regulations govern this. A field measurement (ल्याप्चे) by the Land Revenue Office is the official way to resolve boundary disputes.

What to do: File a complaint at the Land Revenue Office requesting an official field measurement. Both parties will be called to be present during measurement. If the outcome confirms encroachment, the Land Revenue Office can issue an order. If the neighbor still refuses to comply, take the case to the District Court with the official measurement report as key evidence.

## Case: Property fraud — fake sale deed or forged documents

Property fraud is a serious criminal offense in Nepal. Common fraud includes: someone selling property they do not own, using forged lal purjas or citizenship documents, or creating duplicate ownership documents. Victims are typically people who buy land or property in good faith.

What to do: File a criminal complaint at the nearest police station immediately. Also file a civil case at the District Court to have the fraudulent transaction declared void and your rights restored. Request the District Court to freeze/stay the fraudulent registration while the case is pending at the Land Revenue Office. Nepal Police's Economic Crime Investigation Division handles property fraud cases.

## Case: Property inheritance dispute among family members

When someone dies without a will, their property is distributed under the National Civil Code 2074 intestate succession rules. The order of inheritance is: spouse first, then children (sons and daughters equally), then parents. Disputes arise when family members disagree on the division or when someone refuses to participate in partition.

What to do: Try to resolve the matter through a family meeting first, with a neutral mediator if possible. If unresolved, file a partition case (अंशबण्डा मुद्दा) at the District Court. You need to file within the limitation period. Bring the deceased's citizenship, death certificate, land/property documents (lal purja), and your own citizenship proving your family relationship.

## Case: Government acquiring my land (land acquisition)

The Government or local authority can acquire private land for public purposes (roads, schools, hospitals) under the Land Acquisition Act 2034, but only with fair compensation. If you receive an acquisition notice, you have the right to receive market rate or official rate compensation and to challenge the amount if it is unfair.

What to do: Respond to the acquisition notice in writing noting any objection to the compensation amount. Request a formal valuation. If the compensation offered is below market value or below the official rate, file an objection with the acquiring authority and then appeal to the District Court. You should also consult a lawyer since government acquisitions involve strict legal timelines.

## Case: Squatter / unauthorized occupant refusing to vacate my land

If someone is occupying your land without permission (squatter), you can file an eviction case at the District Court. You must be able to prove ownership through your lal purja, field map, and registration documents. The court can issue a possession order directing the occupant to vacate.

What to do: File a civil case at the District Court with your ownership documents. Apply for an interim order (injunction) to stop the occupant from making any improvements or constructions on the land while the case is pending. This prevents the situation from worsening. The case proceeds with a hearing and the court will issue a final possession order if your ownership is established.

## Case: Mortgage — lender threatening to take my property

If you took a loan against your property (registered mortgage) and are having difficulty repaying, the lender does have legal rights to recover through the property — but must follow the proper process under the Bank and Financial Institutions Act (BAFIA) and relevant regulations. A bank or financial institution cannot simply seize your property without court process.

What to do: Communicate with the lender immediately about your repayment difficulties — request a restructuring or extension. If the lender initiates foreclosure, you will receive a notice, and you have the right to respond. Consult a lawyer immediately if you receive a legal notice of foreclosure. In some cases, you can negotiate a settlement, sell the property yourself and repay the loan, or challenge procedural irregularities in the foreclosure process at the District Court.

## Case: Construction defects — builder not fixing problems

If you hired a contractor to build your house and they left defects — cracks in walls, leaking roof, faulty plumbing, structural problems — and are refusing to fix them, you have a legal claim under the contract between you and the builder.

What to do: Document all defects with photographs and written descriptions. Get an independent engineer's report if possible. Send a formal written notice to the contractor specifying the defects and a 30-day deadline to fix them. If they refuse, file a civil case in the District Court for breach of contract and damages. If the contractor disappeared or gave fraudulent quality assurances, a consumer protection complaint at the District Administration Office may also apply.

## Case: Registering property transfer — buying or selling land

Property transfer must be registered at the Land Revenue Office (malpot office) to be legally valid. An unregistered transfer (even with a written agreement and payment) does not legally transfer ownership. The registration process involves: verifying ownership, paying transfer tax, and signing the deed in front of the Land Revenue Officer.

What to do: Both buyer and seller must appear in person at the Land Revenue Office with: citizenship documents, original lal purja, field map, tax clearance certificate, and if applicable, Ward Office NOC. Pay the applicable capital gains tax and registration fees. After registration, the new lal purja will be issued in the buyer's name. Always insist on registered transfer — unregistered sales create massive risks for buyers.

## Case: Co-ownership dispute — joint property conflict

If you co-own property with family members or business partners and they are preventing you from using, renting, or selling your share, you can file for partition of the jointly held property at the District Court. Every co-owner has the right to their proportionate share.

What to do: File a partition case at the District Court. The court can order a physical partition (dividing the property) or an auction partition (selling the property and splitting proceeds) if physical division is not practical. An interim order can prevent the other co-owners from making transactions with the property while the case runs.

---

## Case: Landlord cutting utilities — electricity, water

A landlord who deliberately cuts off your electricity or water supply to force you to vacate is committing an illegal act. This is a form of constructive eviction and harassment. You can report this to the police.

What to do: Document the cutoff (photographs, dates). Send a written notice to the landlord demanding restoration within 24 hours. If not restored, file a complaint at the District Administration Office (CDO) and the nearest police station. The Ward Office can also intervene. This is treated as illegal harassment.

---

## Case: Flat/apartment — builder not handing over possession

If you bought a flat or apartment from a developer and paid in full (or substantially), but the developer is refusing to hand over possession or has delayed beyond the agreed date, you have a strong legal claim under both the contract and the Consumer Protection Act 2075.

What to do: Send a formal written demand with a 30-day deadline for handover. File a consumer protection complaint at the District Administration Office. File a civil breach of contract case at the District Court for specific performance (forcing handover) and damages. If the developer has disappeared or is suspected of fraud, file a criminal complaint at the police station.
