# Contract, Consumer, Banking and Business Cases — Nepal

Jurisdiction: Nepal | Law: Contract Act 2056, Consumer Protection Act 2075, Banking Offense and Punishment Act 2064, Company Act 2063

---

## Case: Paid for a service but it was never provided — service fraud

If you paid for a service (construction, design, event planning, training, software development, coaching, travel booking) and the service was never delivered, you have both a civil claim for breach of contract and potentially a criminal claim for fraud (cheating).

What to do: Send a formal written demand to the service provider giving 15-30 days to either deliver the service or refund your money. Keep all payment records (receipts, bank transfers, screenshots). If they ignore you, file a consumer protection complaint at the District Administration Office (CDO). You can also file a civil breach of contract case in the District Court for the refund plus damages. If you believe it was intentional fraud, file a criminal complaint at the police station.

## Case: Product not delivered after payment — online shopping fraud

If you ordered and paid for a product (through an online shop, social media seller, or marketplace) and it was never delivered, this is both a consumer rights violation and potentially fraud. The Consumer Protection Act 2075 protects buyers in Nepal.

What to do: Contact the seller in writing requesting delivery or refund within 7 days. Screenshot all advertisements, chat conversations, and payment proofs. If no response, file a complaint with Nepal's Consumer Protection Forum at the Department of Commerce, Supplies and Consumer Protection (DOCS&CP), and also at the Cyber Crime Bureau if the seller operated online. You can also file at the CDO (District Administration Office). For amounts over Rs. 50,000, a District Court civil case is also an option.

## Case: Defective product — want a refund or replacement

Under the Consumer Protection Act 2075, you have the right to a repair, replacement, or full refund for any product that is defective, unsafe, or does not match what was advertised. This applies to electronics, vehicles, appliances, food, medicine, and clothing.

What to do: Go back to the seller with the defective product and your receipt. Request a replacement or refund in writing. If refused, file a complaint at the Consumer Rights Desk of the Department of Commerce, Supplies and Consumer Protection. For defective medicines or food, the Department of Food Technology and Quality Control (DFTQC) also has a complaint mechanism. Consumer disputes under Rs. 50,000 are handled quickly by the Consumer Protection Forum.

## Case: Misleading advertisement — product is nothing like advertised

Publishing false or misleading advertisements is an offense under the Consumer Protection Act 2075. This includes fake "before and after" results for health products, exaggerated property claims, deceptive pricing, and hidden charges.

What to do: Keep screenshots or photos of the advertisement and compare them to what you received. File a complaint at the Department of Commerce, Supplies and Consumer Protection. You may also file with the Advertising Council of Nepal. For social media influencer-based fraudulent promotions, the Cyber Bureau may also be relevant.

## Case: Contract breach — other party not performing their agreement

If someone has broken a contract with you (not paying you money owed, not delivering goods or services promised, not doing construction work, not completing a business deal), you have a legal claim under the Contract Act 2056.

What to do: Review the contract carefully — identify the specific term breached. Send a formal legal notice (through a lawyer is more effective) specifying the breach and giving a specific deadline to cure it (typically 15-30 days). Keep all evidence of the contract: signed documents, emails, messages, payment receipts. If unresolved, file a civil case in the District Court. For amounts above Rs. 10 lakh and commercial disputes, the Commercial Court is faster and more specialized.

## Case: Verbal agreement — is it legally enforceable in Nepal?

Under Nepal's Contract Act 2056, a contract does not need to be in writing to be enforceable — verbal (oral) agreements are valid contracts if there was an offer, acceptance, and consideration (exchange of something of value). However, oral contracts are difficult to prove.

What to do: If you made a verbal agreement, try to find any supporting evidence: text messages referencing the deal, emails, witnesses who heard the agreement, bank transfer records showing partial payment, WhatsApp voice messages, or any written notes. Even if formal contract documentation is lacking, courts can consider this supporting evidence. For significant transactions (land, large loans, business deals), always insist on a written and witnessed contract.

## Case: Lent money to someone who won't repay

If you lent money to a friend, relative, or colleague and they are refusing to repay, you have a legal remedy even without a formal loan agreement — provided you can prove the loan happened. Bank transfer records, WhatsApp messages (where they requested or acknowledged the loan), or witnesses are all valid evidence.

What to do: Send a formal written demand for repayment with a 21-day deadline. If ignored, file a civil money claim case in the District Court. For amounts with a signed promissory note (debt receipt) or cheque, you can proceed faster through the Banking Court or Commercial Court. If the borrower issued a cheque that bounced, treat this as a bounced cheque case (criminal remedy also available). Limitation period for money claims is generally 3-5 years.

## Case: Business partner dispute — partner not fulfilling their role

Partnership disputes (disagreement over profit sharing, a partner not doing their work, a partner misusing business funds, or a partner wanting to exit the partnership) are governed by the Partnership Act 2020 and the Contract Act 2056.

What to do: Review your partnership agreement first — most disputes are resolved by the terms already in the agreement. If there is no written partnership deed, establish the facts through financial records, witness testimony, and correspondence. For serious financial misconduct (embezzlement by a partner), file a criminal complaint for fraud as well as a civil case. For general partnership dissolution, file at the Company Registrar's Office (OCR) or the District Court depending on the type of registration.

## Case: Contractor did poor quality construction work

If a contractor built your home or office with poor quality materials, used less materials than billed (for example thinner walls, fewer rods in concrete), or the construction does not meet the agreed specifications, you have a claim for breach of contract and consumer fraud.

What to do: Get an independent civil engineer to assess and document the defects in a written report. Send a formal notice to the contractor specifying defects and demanding remedy in 30 days. If the contractor refuses, file a civil case in the District Court for damages (cost of repair and correction). If the contractor made false representations about materials used, the Consumer Protection Act 2075 and potentially criminal fraud provisions also apply.

## Case: Medical negligence — doctor or hospital caused harm

Medical negligence occurs when a healthcare provider fails to meet the required standard of care, causing injury or death. Under the Medical Treatment Act and Civil Code 2074, patients have the right to compensation for negligence. This is a complex area and requires professional legal and medical advice.

What to do: Preserve all medical records, prescriptions, bills, and documentation. Get an independent medical opinion from a different qualified doctor assessing what went wrong. File a complaint with the Nepal Medical Council (NMC) and also with the Department of Health Services. For compensation, file a civil case in the District Court. For death cases, alert the police as well — depending on the circumstances, it may be criminal negligence too.

## Case: Insurance claim rejected by the company

If your insurance company rejected your claim unfairly (without valid reason, citing exclusions not clearly disclosed, or using procedural grounds to avoid payment), you can challenge this decision.

What to do: Send a written appeal to the insurance company's claims department explaining why the rejection is incorrect. Request the specific policy clause they are relying on. If internal appeal fails, file a complaint with the Insurance Regulatory Authority of Nepal (IRAN). You can also file a civil case in the District Court. Keep all your policy documents, claim submissions, and correspondence.

## Case: Bank overcharged or made unauthorized transaction

If your bank charged you fees not disclosed in your account terms, made an unauthorized transaction, or processed a transaction incorrectly, you have a right to a resolution under the Bank and Financial Institutions Act (BAFIA) and Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) regulations.

What to do: Immediately contact your bank's customer service and dispute the transaction in writing. Request a written response. If the bank does not resolve within 15 days, file a complaint at Nepal Rastra Bank's Consumer Protection Unit (NRB toll-free: 1617). NRB has authority to investigate banks and order refunds. For disputed amounts, also get your bank passbook or statement records as evidence.

## Case: Lost money to investment scam / Ponzi scheme

Investment fraud (promising high returns, pyramid or Ponzi schemes, fake "trading platforms") is unfortunately widespread in Nepal. Many people lose substantial savings to these scams. These are criminal offenses under the Muluki Criminal Code and potentially the Securities Act.

What to do: File a complaint at the Nepal Police (main branch or Cyber Bureau if online). Also file with the Securities Board of Nepal (SEBON) if the scheme claimed to be a licensed investment. Gather all evidence: payment receipts, promotional materials, app screenshots, bank transfer records, and communications. While recovering lost funds is difficult, police action can stop the scheme from victimizing more people, and in some cases frozen assets are distributed to victims.

## Case: Company registration and business disputes

To register a company in Nepal, you register with the Office of the Company Registrar (OCR). Private limited companies need at least one shareholder. Disputes between shareholders, illegal removal of directors, or misuse of company funds are governed by the Company Act 2063.

What to do: For company registration issues, go directly to the OCR. For internal director or shareholder disputes, review the Memorandum of Association (MoA) and Articles of Association (AoA) for dispute resolution procedures. If a director has misappropriated company funds, file a police complaint for fraud. Shareholder derivative suits (on behalf of the company) can be filed in District Court.

## Case: Employment agency / manpower fraud — fees taken for abroad job

Many people in Nepal lose money to fraudulent foreign employment agencies that collect fees but never send them abroad, or send them to jobs very different from what was promised. Foreign employment fraud is a criminal offense under the Foreign Employment Act 2064.

What to do: File a complaint at the Foreign Employment Promotion Board (FEPB) and the Department of Foreign Employment. Also file a criminal complaint at the police station. Many cases are handled by Nepal Police's Anti-Human Trafficking Bureau. Keep all documents: receipt for fees paid, the license number of the agency, the job agreement, and any promotional materials. Compensation can be claimed from the licensed agency's security deposit held with the government.

## Case: Consumer rights — expired products sold in shops

Selling expired food, medicine, cosmetics, or any product past its expiry date is an offense under the Consumer Protection Act 2075 and the Food Act 2023. You can report this even if you were not personally harmed — it is a public interest concern.

What to do: Photograph the expired product and note the shop name and location. Report to the local Ward Office and the Department of Commerce, Supplies and Consumer Protection. For expired medicines specifically, the Department of Drug Administration (DDA) is the right authority. Inspectors can raid the shop and levy fines. If you consumed an expired product and became ill, you may also have a personal injury compensation claim.
